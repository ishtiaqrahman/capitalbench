The macroeconomic environment exhibits varying dynamics across inflation, labor, growth, consumption, and real estate sectors according to the latest releases from principal statistical agencies. The United States Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) reported that the Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers (CPI-U) increased by 0.6 percent on a seasonally adjusted month-over-month basis in April 2026\.2 This reading matches market expectations and represents a deceleration from the 0.9 percent month-over-month surge recorded in March 2026, which was the largest sequential increase since 2022\.3 On an unadjusted year-over-year basis, the headline inflation rate reached 3.8 percent in April 2026, marking an acceleration from the 3.3 percent annual rate observed in March and registering the highest twelve-month advance since May 2023\.1 Energy prices served as a primary upward component, climbing 3.8 percent in April after a 10.9 percent spike the previous month.3 Within the energy index, gasoline prices advanced 5.4 percent month-over-month and 28.4 percent year-over-year, largely reflecting the effects of the Iran conflict and the closure of the Strait of Hormuz.1 Food prices also climbed, recording a 0.5 percent monthly increase in April following a flat reading in March.2 This included a 0.7 percent increase in the index for food at home and a 0.2 percent increase in the index for food away from home.2
The Core Consumer Price Index, which excludes the volatile food and energy categories, advanced 0.4 percent month-over-month in April 2026, surpassing the consensus expectation of a 0.3 percent increase and doubling the 0.2 percent rate recorded in March.3 This represented the largest one-month core print since January 2025\.3 On an annual basis, the Core CPI increased by 2.8 percent, up from the 2.6 percent year-over-year rate observed in March.1 Driving the core acceleration was the shelter index, which rose 0.6 percent in April compared to a 0.3 percent increase in March, representing a 3.3 percent advance year-over-year.1 Additional indices recording monthly increases included household furnishings and operations, personal care, apparel, education, and airline fares, the latter of which rose 2.8 percent month-over-month.1 Conversely, price indices for new vehicles, communication, and medical care experienced declines during the observation period.3 Services, less energy services, contributed 0.301 percentage points to the overall 0.6 percent headline increase.6
The Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA) released the Personal Income and Outlays report, detailing the Personal Consumption Expenditures (PCE) price index. The headline PCE price index increased 3.50 percent year-over-year in March 2026, which follows year-over-year changes of 2.8 percent in February 2026, 2.9 percent in January 2026, and 2.9 percent in December 2025\.7 The Core PCE price index, excluding food and energy, rose 3.20 percent year-over-year in March 2026, accelerating from the 3.0 percent rate in February, the 3.1 percent rate in January, and the 3.0 percent rate in December 2025\.9 On a sequential basis, the Core PCE price index increased 0.30 percent month-over-month in March 2026, following a 0.40 percent increase in February.11 In tandem with the price data, the BEA reported that personal income decreased by $18.2 billion, or 0.1 percent, in March.40 Disposable personal income, representing personal income less personal current taxes, increased by $142.5 billion, or 0.6 percent.8 Personal consumption expenditures (PCE) advanced by $195.4 billion, reflecting a 0.9 percent month-over-month increase.8 Personal outlays, which sum PCE, personal interest payments, and personal current transfer payments, increased by $198.6 billion in March, while personal saving reached $857.3 billion, equating to a personal saving rate of 3.6 percent of disposable personal income.8
Wholesale inflation exhibited pronounced acceleration according to the BLS Producer Price Index (PPI) release. The PPI for final demand advanced 1.4 percent on a seasonally adjusted basis in April 2026, surpassing the 0.7 percent advance in March and the 0.6 percent advance in February.12 The April sequential increase constitutes the largest advance since the index rose 1.7 percent in March 2022\.13 On an unadjusted basis, the final demand index escalated 6.0 percent over the twelve months ending in April 2026, the largest annual increase since the 6.4 percent move in December 2022\.13 The primary driver of the monthly final demand increase was a 1.2 percent advance in the index for final demand services, the largest such increase since March 2022\.13 Within the services segment, two-thirds of the broad-based advance was attributed to a 2.7 percent expansion in margins for final demand trade services.13 The indexes for final demand transportation and warehousing services increased by 5.0 percent, and final demand services less trade, transportation, and warehousing rose by 0.1 percent.13 Prices for final demand goods moved higher by 2.0 percent.13 The core PPI measure, tracking final demand less foods, energy, and trade services, increased by 0.6 percent in April, standing 4.4 percent higher over the twelve months ending in April, marking the largest year-over-year expansion since February 2023\.13 Energy prices within the producer framework were reported to be up 22.7 percent over the year ended April 2026\.14
